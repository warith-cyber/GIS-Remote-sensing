01 / The challenge

Air risk is not distributed equally.

Klang's industrial economy and dense transport network sit beside homes, schools, hospitals and public spaces. City-wide averages can hide the neighbourhoods where exposure and vulnerability overlap.

01

Urban exposure

Road traffic, industrial activity and freight movement create spatially uneven pollution pressures.

02

Human vulnerability

Children, patients and residents require closer attention where sensitive land uses cluster.

03

Smarter sensing

Low-cost IoT nodes can fill monitoring gaps when they are placed by evidence, not convenience.

Design question

Where should sensors go to observe both the causes of pollution and its greatest human consequences?

07 / Discussion

What the spatial pattern tells us.

01

Corridor effects

Continuous road and freight corridors can create connected exposure zones rather than isolated hotspots.

02

Vulnerability changes priority

A moderate source score can become a high monitoring priority when hospitals, schools or dense housing are nearby.

03

A network, not a single point

High-ranked sites support targeted nodes, while medium-ranked sites provide background context and spatial continuity.

Planning principle

Optimise for explanatory coverage: sensors should capture gradients between source-dominated, population-dominated and background environments.

08 / Conclusion

From spatial evidence to an accountable monitoring network.

GIS-based MCDM provides a defensible way to prioritise scarce IoT sensors in Klang. By balancing emission sources with vulnerable populations, the model directs monitoring toward places where better information can support better public-health decisions.

Recommendation

Deploy an initial sensor network across high-suitability zones, validate conditions through field surveys, then adapt weights and locations using observed air-quality data.
